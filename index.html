Arguably the most important factor for finding earth like and just habitable planets is its location withtin the habitable zone. The habitable zone is an area beggining and ending some distance away from a star in which for the most part, water can exist in a liquid form. Liquid water is vital to life as we know it as it serves as a medium for chemical reactions to occur in, more complex compounds to form, among other things.
Fortunately for us NASA has an equation to find the inner and outer range of the habitable zone for G2 stars.

We will be using three new rows for our purposes. HZinner and HZouter will be used to store the inner and outer reach of the habitable zone. Using a simple logical check we will also see if the planets orbit falls within the habitable zone.

    G2.at[i,'HZouter'] = 1.77*math.sqrt(G2.at[i,'st_rad']**2 * (G2.at[i,'st_teff']/5780)**4)
    if (G2.at[i,'pl_orbsmax'] < G2.at[i,'HZinner']) or (G2.at[i,'pl_orbsmax'] > G2.at[i,'HZouter']):
        G2.at[i,'habitable'] = 0

Conclusion¶

Though this analysis went well it's important to keep in mind that the info we got here is very likely to change in the future. Life on moons such as Europa could be confirmed, which would expand the range of planets we would be interested in. Analysis of exoplanet atmospheres could confirm or deny the possibility of life forming on them in the first place. Despite these facts, It's still meaningful to at least keep in mind some of the factors which go into habitability, and how the points of interest expand over time so that when a new discovery is made, perhaps you'll be able to extract the data you need without more help from the tutorial.
