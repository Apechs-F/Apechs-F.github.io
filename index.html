Arguably the most important factor for finding earth like and just habitable planets is its location withtin the habitable zone. The habitable zone is an area beggining and ending some distance away from a star in which for the most part, water can exist in a liquid form. Liquid water is vital to life as we know it as it serves as a medium for chemical reactions to occur in, more complex compounds to form, among other things. We will be using three new rows for our purposes. HZinner and HZouter will be used to store the inner and outer reach of the habitable zone. Using a simple logical check we will also see if the planets orbit falls within the habitable zone. Fortunately for us NASA has an equation to find the inner and outer range of the habitable zone for G2 stars.

G2['HZinner'] = np.nan
G2['HZouter'] = np.nan
G2['habitable'] = 1
for i, row in (G2.iterrows()):
    G2.at[i,'HZinner'] = 0.75*math.sqrt(G2.at[i,'st_rad']**2 * (G2.at[i,'st_teff']/5780)**4) #calculating the bounds
    G2.at[i,'HZouter'] = 1.77*math.sqrt(G2.at[i,'st_rad']**2 * (G2.at[i,'st_teff']/5780)**4)
    if (G2.at[i,'pl_orbsmax'] < G2.at[i,'HZinner']) or (G2.at[i,'pl_orbsmax'] > G2.at[i,'HZouter']): #checking if the planet falls within
        G2.at[i,'habitable'] = 0

Part 2: Lowering our standards¶

So clearly taking a very earth centered approach is not the way to go. But even if we go to the absolute limits of what can be considered habitable it would still be difficult concretely state that was a planet was habitable due to the ammount of factors which play into the equation, a lot of which we simply do not know. We can however account for as many factors as possible given the data, and for the sake of our experiment, take on the most optimistic view possible.

Beggining our analysis, we first have to talk about main sequence stars. 90% of the stars in our universe are main sequence stars, meaning they fuse hydrogen to form helium, like our sun does. Studying these main sequence stars astronomers managed to come to the conclussion that a main sequence stars' temperature, color, and luminosity are all related. All of these variables playing a role in our calculation of the habitable zone and as such are of great interest to us.

For right now, I want to see the spread of discovered exoplanets across varying stars.

One other equation which I want to take a look at before we wrap up is the Drake equation. This equation is a theoretical prediction of the number of advanced extraterrestrial civilization with the technology to reach out to us. Initially made to stir up dialogue during some of the first SETI meetings it acts as a fairly good summary of some of the factors we need to consider in our search for extraterrestrial intelligent life. One of the factors in this equation is the average number of planets that can potentially support life per star that has planets. So we will be comparing our results using the habitable planet data we got to a relatively optimistic calculation.
